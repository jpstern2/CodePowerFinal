I. Introduction

Objective:
The objective of this proposal is to outline a comprehensive strategy for adopting web development practices at the Wisconsin Maritime Museum, focusing on ensuring accessibility for all users. This strategy aims to make the museum's online presence inclusive, ensuring that it meets WCAG 2.0 or 2.1 AA standards and offers an equitable experience for every visitor, regardless of ability.

Context:
Web accessibility is not merely a technical requirement; it is a fundamental aspect of ensuring that digital spaces are open and inclusive to all. According to the World Health Organization, over 1 billion people, or 15% of the world's population, experience some form of disability. Despite this, a significant portion of the web remains inaccessible to these individuals. The museum sector is no exception—studies show that many museum websites fail to meet basic accessibility standards, thereby excluding a substantial audience. This proposal addresses the need for accessible web development at the Wisconsin Maritime Museum, focusing on creating an inclusive digital environment that reflects the museum's commitment to public education and historical preservation.

II. Institutional Overview

Institution Description:
The Wisconsin Maritime Museum, located in Manitowoc, Wisconsin, is a premier institution dedicated to preserving and interpreting the maritime history of the Great Lakes. Founded in 1970, the museum houses a vast collection of artifacts, including ship models, historical documents, and the World War II submarine USS Cobia. The museum's mission is to engage the public with the rich maritime heritage of the region, offering educational programs, interactive exhibits, and research opportunities.

Users and Stakeholders:
The museum's stakeholders encompass a diverse group of users, each with distinct needs and expectations. Understanding these user groups is critical to developing a website that meets their varied requirements:

  • General Public Users:
    This group includes tourists, local visitors, and casual learners who seek an engaging, visually appealing experience. Their needs revolve around easy navigation, compelling content, and interactive features that bring maritime history to life. These users may not have a deep understanding of maritime history but are eager to explore and learn through the museum’s offerings.
  • Academic Researchers and Librarians:
    These users require detailed, accurate access to the museum's collections and archives. Their needs include advanced search capabilities, metadata-rich content, and access to high-resolution images and documents. Researchers may also seek to download datasets or use geospatial information to analyze historical maritime routes and events.
  • Educators and Students:
    This group looks for educational resources that align with curriculum standards. They need access to multimedia content, downloadable lesson plans, and interactive tools that facilitate learning about maritime history.

Information and Technology Needs:
The museum's information needs are centered around showcasing its exhibits, providing educational resources, and engaging a broad audience. The technological needs include a robust, accessible website that serves as a digital extension of the museum. The website must handle various data types, including text, images, videos, and interactive elements, while ensuring that all content is accessible to users with disabilities.

Representation of Institutional Data Types:
The museum's collections include a wide range of data types, such as artifact metadata, historical documents, photographs, and geospatial data. Effective representation of these data types on the website is crucial for accessibility. For example, the museum might use interactive maps to display shipwreck locations, overlaid with historical data about each event. Additionally, the website could feature virtual tours of the museum, allowing users to explore exhibits from their devices. These features must be designed with accessibility in mind, ensuring that all users can engage with the content regardless of their abilities.

III. Technology Overview

Description of Web Development Tools:
The website will be developed using a combination of HTML, CSS, and JavaScript, with web frameworks such as Bootstrap for UI/UX design and ARIA (Accessible Rich Internet Applications) to enhance accessibility. Additionally, the use of WebGL for 3D graphics and Leaflet for interactive maps will be explored to create a dynamic and immersive experience.

Potential Implementation:
The website will be built with accessibility as a core principle from the ground up. This includes the following features:

  • Interactive Geospatial Data:
    Using Leaflet, the website will feature interactive maps that allow users to explore maritime routes, shipwrecks, and other historical events. These maps will be accessible, offering keyboard navigation and alternative text descriptions for all interactive elements.
  • 3D Models and Virtual Tours:
    Leveraging WebGL, the site will include 3D models of artifacts, such as the USS Cobia, and virtual tours of the museum. These features will be complemented with descriptive audio and text alternatives to ensure accessibility for users with visual impairments.
  • Responsive Design:
    The website will be fully responsive, ensuring that it functions well on all devices, from desktops to smartphones. This includes using Bootstrap’s grid system to create a flexible layout that adjusts to different screen sizes.

IV. The Why

Informative Aspect:
Accessible web development is essential for creating an inclusive digital environment. For many users, particularly those with disabilities, an inaccessible website can be a significant barrier to engaging with content. In the context of museums, this means that valuable educational and cultural resources may be out of reach for a significant portion of the population. By implementing accessible web development practices, the Wisconsin Maritime Museum can ensure that all users, regardless of ability, can access and interact with its online content. This not only aligns with legal standards but also fulfills the museum’s mission to educate and engage a diverse audience.

Persuasive Aspect:
Adopting accessible web development practices will position the museum as a leader in inclusivity and digital accessibility. This leadership role can enhance the museum’s reputation, attract more visitors, and potentially open up new opportunities for partnerships and funding. Moreover, an accessible website can improve overall visitor satisfaction, leading to increased engagement and repeat visits. A study by the Nielsen Norman Group found that accessible websites often provide a better user experience for all users, not just those with disabilities. By making accessibility a priority, the museum can ensure that it provides the best possible experience for every visitor.

Case Study:
Consider the example of the Smithsonian National Museum of Natural History, which implemented a comprehensive accessibility initiative for its digital content. The museum’s website now includes features such as audio descriptions, keyboard navigation, and alternative text for images. As a result, the Smithsonian saw a significant increase in engagement from users with disabilities, as well as positive feedback from all user groups. This case demonstrates the tangible benefits of prioritizing accessibility in web development.

V. Code And Power

Identification of Biases:
Biases in web development can manifest in various ways, from the choice of content to the design of user interfaces. At the Wisconsin Maritime Museum, potential biases might include the prioritization of certain historical narratives over others or the presentation of data in ways that are inaccessible to some users. For example, non-semantic HTML elements can create barriers for screen reader users, while certain data visualizations might inadvertently emphasize information that aligns with the museum’s institutional perspective, potentially marginalizing alternative narratives.

Impact on Stakeholders:
These biases can disproportionately affect users with disabilities, preventing them from fully engaging with the museum’s digital content. For instance, if a data visualization is not accessible to screen readers, users with visual impairments may be unable to access critical information. Similarly, if the website prioritizes certain narratives, it may alienate users who are interested in exploring different aspects of maritime history. This can lead to exclusion and marginalization, undermining the museum’s mission to provide an inclusive and educational experience for all.

Theoretical Considerations:
From a theoretical standpoint, biases in web development reflect broader societal inequities. Critical theory, particularly the work of scholars like Michel Foucault, emphasizes the ways in which power dynamics shape knowledge production and dissemination. In the context of the Wisconsin Maritime Museum, this means that the design and content of the website must be carefully considered to avoid reinforcing existing power structures and to promote equity and inclusion. This involves not only making the website accessible but also ensuring that the content is representative of diverse perspectives and that all users have the opportunity to engage with the museum’s digital offerings.

VI. Ethical Web Development Strategies

Bias Mitigation:
To mitigate biases, the website will adhere to the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.1 AA standards. This includes using semantic HTML, providing text alternatives for non-text content, and ensuring keyboard accessibility. Additionally, the museum will engage in inclusive design practices, such as conducting stakeholder consultations and usability testing with diverse user groups. These practices will help identify potential biases and ensure that the website meets the needs of all users.

Accessibility Testing:
Accessibility testing will be an ongoing process throughout the website’s development. Tools such as Axe Accessibility Checker and WAVE will be used to identify and rectify accessibility issues. These tools provide detailed reports on potential problems, such as missing alt text or insufficient color contrast, allowing the development team to make necessary adjustments. Additionally, the museum will conduct user testing with individuals who have disabilities, ensuring that the website is truly accessible in real-world scenarios.

Ongoing Accessibility Monitoring:
Once the website is live, ongoing monitoring will be essential to maintain accessibility. This will include regular audits using automated tools, as well as user feedback mechanisms that allow visitors to report accessibility issues. The museum will also commit to periodic updates to ensure that the website continues to meet the latest accessibility standards and best practices.

VII. The Space between the Code

User Experience (UX) Considerations:
User experience (UX) is closely intertwined with accessibility. A well-designed website not only meets accessibility standards but also provides an intuitive and enjoyable experience for all users. To achieve this, the website will incorporate UX design principles such as simplicity, consistency, and user control. For example, the navigation will be designed to be easy to understand and use, with clear labels and logical groupings of content. Additionally, the website will include features such as adjustable font sizes and color contrast settings, allowing users to customize the interface to their needs.
