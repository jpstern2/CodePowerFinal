Individual Technology Adoption Strategy Proposal

Wisconsin Maritime Museum

I.                   Introduction

This proposal aims to support users of the Wisconsin Maritime Museum in analyzing the data available in its online collection. The museum’s main goal is to engage and educate the public on topics related to the Great Lakes, Wisconsin’s maritime history, and ships. This proposal focuses on the development of a tool that uses web scraping and geocoding technologies in Python to create visualizations of the online collection metadata, including maps with the location of photographs.

II.                Institutional profile

The Wisconsin Maritime Museum is an institution located in Manitowoc, in the state of Wisconsin, United States. It was created in 1969 – initially, its name was Manitowoc Submarine Memorial Association. Focused on the maritime history of Wisconsin and the Great Lakes, the museum attracts a significant number of visitors. As shown in their 2023 report, more than 30,000 people visited the museum[1]. The staff comprises 11 employees, not including volunteers, and the board of trustees has 12 members.

The institution’s main activities are exhibits, field trips, and outreach events. Users include students, researchers, and people interested in the subject. Even though each group of users has specific data needs, this tool could be helpful for all of them. For example, it could aid researchers to quickly grasp what is available given their needs, such as which places are documented. It could also be useful for the general public to consume information in a more visual way, without too many details that could be overwhelming.

The online collection has a fraction of the museum’s collection, with a strong emphasis on photographs. Each photograph record has its own metadata. Not all fields are complete in each of the items, but there is still a significant amount of information available. Data types encountered in the online collection are strings for fields such as description, search terms, object name, and people. The description field, specifically, can have much information, which could lead to different types of analysis using text analysis, for instance. The example of a photograph record below has information that could be used in research about safety committees. Search terms, object name, and people have more concise texts, as seen in the example below.

Example of photograph record

The field date is usually in the MM/DD/YYYY format, although sometimes it has only a year, or an approximate date, written as a string. Similarly, the field accession generally denotes the accession date, but sometimes it can be an id in a string format.

The development of a tool that could process this data is important for the users, but it would also require skilled employees to create it. There is a need to work with texts, incomplete dates, and fields with different data types, for example.

 

III.             Technology Overview

In order to understand the information regarding the museum’s online collection of photographs, there is a need to access all of the metadata fields and be able to sort the ones we are interested in. Web scraping is a great tool to accomplish this, as it allows us to extract data from websites.

This proposal’s tool aims to gather the photographs’ metadata and parse location data to create maps. It would help researchers to see the geographic information from a new perspective, and it could possibly bring some new questions that would not come up through the visualization of one photograph at a time, such as “What are the most documented areas in the museum’s photograph collection?”. This would help to fulfill one of the declared purposes of the museum, which is to “create personal and meaningful connections with our waterways”[2]. It is interesting to analyze the big picture of the collection, and to understand its strengths and weaknesses – the lack of information is also information.

 

IV.             Issues of power / Power mitigation

This tool can be used to understand the strengths of the collection, such as the items, periods, persons, or places that are more documented. It can also be used to understand the weaknesses of the collection, or what information is missing. A potential bias resulting from this tool is that, since some of the items lack specific metadata fields, it would not be an accurate portrait of the collection. If this technology is used for the purposes of analyzing the people in the photographs, for instance, it would also result in biased information, as there are photographs where they are not identified.

One feature of the technology in this proposal is that it allows us to measure bias in the collection. We can have an overall understanding of the historical periods represented, the ports in Wisconsin (and other regions, especially around the Great Lakes), the people and companies involved in the maritime industry, and so on. Having this information also leads to the acknowledgement of the information that is missing in the collections, which could prompt the staff to try to acquire documents from places, periods or people that are underrepresented.

While the tool developed for this proposal focuses on the geographic component, there is other information that could also be summarized using text and data analysis. For example, some of the long descriptions for each photograph could be analyzed to identify important topics of the period being represented. Another example would be regarding the people represented in the photographs. We see many indications of “sponsors”, but there are other professions/descriptions that could be in the photographs, such as those related to crew members. It would be interesting to analyze this feature more systematically from the information available in the metadata.

One way to address issues of lack of information would be to have well documented datasheets for the datasets. An adaptation of the model presented by Gebru, Timnit et al. (2021) would be a good solution, with emphasis on what is missing and why. It is interesting to add that a significant part of the collection was donated to the museum, which explains the lack of more information. Nevertheless, having a datasheet with all the details available regarding the collection process could help explain the gaps in the visualizations, and give more context.

The Date field can also result in biased information, as the dates mentioned are not standardized (some variations are the year, the full date, “circa”, etc.). This tool could be adjusted to focus only on years, for instance, or decades.
